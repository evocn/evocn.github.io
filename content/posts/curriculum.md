Computer education is DIY.

Classes teach basics, point out the most popular methods, frameworks.
The learning happens inbetween.

I've held a love-hate relationship with this reality for the four years I've
spent programming.
On the one hand, information is free, knowledge is out there, and you can teach
yourself anything these days.
On the other hand, it's discouraging. My best resources are archaic books packed
to the gills with information that you can't effectively search through, along
with Google, the mother of my despair, which you can effectively search through,
but is so packed with garbage that it boggles the mind.

Whatever my feelings on the matter, it's here to stay.
The only cure for such a scenario is action.

I have a fine opportunity coming up. I'll be taking a leave of absence from
school for the Winter quarter and learning on my own. I've done this before,
with mixed results.
Therefore, I know what lies ahead of me – the problems I'll face.

For some reason there is no guidance on this front from any blogs, classes, etc.
This means that either people globally suck at this, or it's one of those things
in life that must be learned the hard way. So be it!

However, I might as well enumerate the obstacles you'll encounter when you set
out to do something like this.
My wisdom can help you to better prepare yourself for setting out on a journey
of your own.

# The problems with self-learning
**Motivation**
* Motivation does not exist.

**Wasting Time**
* When you learn on your own, you don't know what's central and what's fluff.
* When you take a course in school, your professor knows which chapters of the
  textbook to teach.

**Getting Help**
* Programming stuck is the worst kind of stuck you can be in.
* Most of the time when you're learning like this you're in a new, unfamiliar
  programming environment (e.g. trying to learn a new language, algorithm, API).
* Read documentation, search Google... It doesn't matter how good you are at
  making the right query and searching the state space, these things take time.
  A teacher (human being) will always be quicker, more adaptable.

For this Winter, I've devised some strategies to negate these problems and have
successfully applied each to my own life in some way.

# The solutions
**Motivation**
* Those who succeed build systems such that they do not require motivation, 
  those who fail do not.
* Building habits is more essential to success in this field than developing
  intrinsic or extrinsic motivators.
* Whatever you do, finish quickly. Momentum is huge. I recently finished a big
  [movie project](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iTHIrhCDMW8), and the joy of
  finishing something like that is immense. It gave me the energy I needed to
  make my [best movie yet](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=54FBdRIYEFE)
  literally the next week.

**Wasting Time**
* Be extremely discerning about your goal in pursuing the knowledge. For a
  primer on this, watch 
  [this Jon Blow talk](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SqFu5O-oPmU).
  * If you have no goal, don't try to learn things on your own. Find a
    structured environment where you can relax and focus your energies on
    discovery.
* Focus on products. It's a [neutral indicator of value](https://sive.rs/mn1)
  which means that you can actually judge whether something is worth doing by
  whether or not it is load-bearing.
  * For instance, when your product is on creating a fun game for people to
    play, do not waste your time (or worse, your energy) focusing on anything
    other than the core gameplay until you have a viable product.
* All learning can be classified into two groups: *Exploration* and *Performance*.
  We want Performance most of the time when learning on our own, because without structure,
  things fall apart.
  * When you do decide to focus on Exploration, make it intentional and
    explicit.

**Getting Help**
* Find the best resources you possibly can. Buy the best textbooks, find the
  most concise tutorials.
* Seek out help in unconventional ways. If you live near a university, approach
  professors with questions about their subject areas even when you aren't in
  their classes. They love talking and Office Hours are usually criminally
  underpopulated anyways.
* Become laser-focused on targeting for specific information, asking pinpoint
  questions, and rejecting your resources when you don't find something within a
  few minutes. Better to drop a feature than spend days looking for answers on
  some forum on the third page of Google search results.

# Endnote
And as a final note, there is a single guiding principle overriding all of these
factors: ***Incremental Growth***.
You must believe completely in Incremental Growth.
If you do not, you will live your life in fear.
Believe that if you put in a little bit of time every day – a couple of dollars
in the mason jar on the mantle every paycheck – you will succeed.

But don't take my word for it!
Let Ovid tell you what's up.
> "Dripping water hollows out stone, not through force but through persistence"

Curriculum

Computer education is DIY.

Classes teach basics, point out the most popular methods, frameworks. The learning happens inbetween them.

I’ve held a love-hate relationship with this reality for the four years I’ve spent programming.

On the one hand, information is free, knowledge is out there, and you can teach yourself anything these days.

On the other hand, it’s discouraging. My best resources are archaic books packed to the gills with information that you can’t effectively search through, along with Google, the mother of my despair, which you can effectively search through, but is so packed with garbage that it boggles the mind.

Opportunity

Whatever my feelings on the matter, it’s here to stay. The only cure for such a scenario is action.

I have a fine opportunity coming up. I’ll be taking a leave of absence from school for the Winter quarter and learning on my own.

I’ve done this before, Therefore, I know some things about the problems I’ll face.

For some reason there is no guidance on this front from any blogs, classes, etc. This means that either people globally suck at this, or it’s one of those things in life that must be learned the hard way. So be it!

However, I might as well enumerate the obstacles you’ll encounter when you set out to do something like this.

The problems with self-learning

The three main problems with self-learning are generally threefold:

First, one must ensure that they do the work. This turns out to be hard, and doesn’t correlate exactly with levels of motivation.

Second, one must use their time to the fullest, which is difficult in self-learning. Many days will be typically spent searching for a resource or simply learning something useless or tangential, since you don’t know any better.

Third, getting stuck is crippling to the solo learning, while it is a minor setback to the school-goer.

Here’s a dive into each.

Motivation

Motivation does not exist.

Solution

  • Those who succeed build systems such that they do not require motivation.
  • Whatever you do, finish quickly. Momentum is huge.
    • I recently finished a big movie project, and the joy of finishing something like that is immense. It gave me the energy I needed to make my best movie yet literally the next week. I believe this momentum is almost entirely what great creators use.

Wasting Time

  • When you learn on your own, you don’t know what’s central and what’s fluff.
  • When you take a course in school, your professor knows which chapters of the textbook to teach.

Solution

  • Be extremely discerning about your goal in pursuing the knowledge.
    • If you have no goal, don’t try to learn things on your own. It’s not worth it. Find a structured environment where you can relax and focus your energies on discovery.
  • Focus on products.
    • When your product is on creating a fun game for people to play, do not waste your time (or worse, your energy) focusing on anything other than the core gameplay until you have a viable product.
  • There are two modes of being: Exploration and Performance.
    • We want Performance most of the time when learning on our own, because without structure, things fall apart.
    • When you do decide to focus on Exploration, make it intentional and explicit.

Getting Help

  • Being stuck while programming is the most depressing thing you can undergo.
  • Much of the time you’re in a new, unfamiliar programming environment (e.g. trying to learn a new language, algorithm, API), rather than a familiar one.
  • Read documentation, search Google… It doesn’t matter how good you are at making the right query and searching the state space, these things take time. A teacher (human being) will always be quicker, more adaptable.

Solution

  • Find the best resources you possibly can. Buy the best textbooks, find the most concise tutorials.
  • Seek out help in unconventional ways. If you live near a university, approach professors with questions about their subject areas even when you aren’t in their classes. They love talking and office hours are usually criminally underpopulated anyways. Ask whatever experts you know.
  • Become laser-focused on targeting for specific information, asking pinpoint questions, and rejecting your resources when you don’t find something within a few minutes. Better to drop a feature than spend days looking for answers on some forum on the third page of Google search results.

Endnote

And as a final note, there is a single guiding principle overriding all of these factors:

Incremental Growth.

You must believe completely in Incremental Growth. If you do not, you will live your life in fear. Do not despair in a lesson left unlearned, a program left unfinished at the end of the day. Believe that if you put in a little bit of time every day – a couple of dollars in the mason jar on the mantle every paycheck – you will succeed.

But don’t take my word for it! Let Ovid tell you what’s up.

“Dripping water hollows out stone, not through force but through persistence”

Good luck with your learnings. I’ll be right in there with you.

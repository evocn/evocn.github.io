Bones

Bones is a Tabletop Role-Playing Game I will be running soon.

Why another RPG?

Violence is misrepresented and misunderstood.

In films, stories, games, and even in the general public consciousness, what we call “violence” is a pale echo of anything real.

For instance, in film, a skilled knight might defeat four or five soldiers in battle. This is insanity! Imagine being in a room with even two enemy combatants. Think of the terror of knowing you’re outnumbered, of knowing that one misstep could mean an agonizing blow, a painful death, a loss of everything you hold dear. I almost never see Role-Playing games tap into this real-life core of violence, of what it means to kill something, of what it means to put your life on the line for something you desire.

This is the kind of simple drama I seek to create with this game.

Now, I’m not setting out to right the wrongs of society and culture. I actually just think that real-life violence is far more rich and compelling a subject than the form of violence which our media focuses on.

I think we can make a really compelling game about how violence really is. I want to give it a real, earnest effort. I want us to explore it the best we can.

Proposal

I propose a set of short games, exploring a variety of topics and characters. The games involve one to three players, depending on the subject.

They take place in a single setting, in vicinity to one another, but are mostly unrelated to one another. Each game is 1-3 sessions, based on the subject.

My inspirations are the following. I want to run a game which is:

  • raw, gritty, and grounded in the physical reality of violence, like First Blood.
  • pulp-y, quick, mysterious, and tense, like Conan, the Barbarian.
  • reverent, sometimes melancholy, and sometimes unbelievably beautiful, like The Dying Earth.

Rules

The Basics

There are 5 attributes which govern characters, which typically range from -3 to +3.

Attribute Examples
Agility Dodging, sneaking, leaping.
Strength Pushing, pulling, climbing, wrestling, melee weapons.
Clarity Perception, ranged weapons.
Knowledge Lore, survival, mending of injuries, use of technology.
Grit Ability to withstand pain and stay alive when injured.

This is a D6-based game. Everything in the game involves rolling a D6, adding a given modifier to the roll, and testing that result against some Difficulty Class (DC).

Difficulty DC
Easy 3
Moderate 5
Hard 7
Impossible 9

Violence

Combat

Each round:

  • Move (across a room).
  • Do an action.

There’s a few combat stats.

  • Avoid is determined by your Agility plus 4. That’s what the enemy must roll to hit you.
  • Armor is determined by what you’re defending yourself with. That reduces the amount of damage done to you.
  • Wounds capacity is determined by your Grit + 2. That’s how many Wounds you can take before passing out.

Attacking

To Hit

Roll D6. Add Strength to melee weapons, Add Clarity to ranged weapons. If the total meets the enemy’s Avoid, you hit!

Damage

In this game, we don’t track Hit Points, instead we track Wounds. Roll damage, subtract the target’s Armor, then determine the Wounds inflicted with the following chart:

Damage Wound
0-3 None
4-7 Minor
8+ Major

Wounds

There are two kinds of Wounds. Minor Wounds have a chance to be mended with time and rest. Permanent Wounds are forever.

Each wound has consequences. We roll on this table when a character takes a wound.

(d6) Minor Wound
1 Concussion. -2 to Knowledge, Clarity.
2 Slipped Rib. -1 to Strength, Clarity.
3 Broken Arm. -1 hands.
4 Broken Foot. -2 Agility. Half speed.
5 Agonizing Gash. -1 Strength and Agility.
6 Wind Knocked out. Stunned for a turn.
(D6) Permanent Wound Effect
1 Lose an eye. -1 to Clarity.
2 Lose a hand. -1 hands.
3 Lose a foot. -2 Agility. Half speed.
4 Shredded Organs. -1 Grit.
5 Brain Damage. -2 to Knowledge, Clarity.
6 Death’s Door All further Wounds are fatal.

When a character takes more Wounds than they can handle, they pass out. They can be carried to safety and stabilized.

For instance, if you have a Wound Capacity of 2, and you take a third wound, you pass out.

An unconscious character who takes another Wound dies.

Recovery

Characters who have time and resources to recover can attempt to mend their Wounds.

Each rest, once for each Minor Wound, any character can roll a DC 5 Knowledge check to mend a Wound. Alternatively, for your own wounds, you can attempt a DC 6 Grit check to manage a wound yourself.

If your character gets proper rest for two weeks, all Minor Injuries are healed.

Critical

On a weapon’s maximum damage output, you score a critical. You may choose to sunder a piece of the enemy’s equipment, or deal a Major Wound regardless of the overall damage number.

Sunder

In this game, equipment can break, rendering it unusable. Some combatants will attempt to break your weapons and armor, leaving you vulnerable. Also, you can choose to deliberately risk your own equipment in order to avoid taking wounds.

When some force would attempt to wound you, you can choose to sunder any piece of equipment on your person to reduce the severity of the wound inflicted.

Note that you declare your action to sunder a piece of equipment after the opponent has determined that they hit, but before they have rolled to determine how much of a wound it would cause. You might waste a sunder on a hit which wouldn’t have wounded you!

Some equipment can reduce wounds more than others.

  • Sunder 1 reduces a Major wound to a Minor wound, or a Minor wound to nothing.
  • Sunder 2 reduces all wounds to nothing. You can find an equipment’s Sunder score in the charts below.

After you sunder a piece of equipment, it’s trash.

Here’s an example of all this in action:

Rog comes crashing down on a Goblin using his Longsword. He rolls a d6 (gets a 5) and adds his Strength score (2). The Goblin’s got a 3 in Agility, so his 5 (Rolled) + 2 (Strength) = 7 meets (and therefore beats) the Goblin’s 4 (Default avoidance bonus) + 3 (Agility) = 7.

Rog rolls damage. An 8! On his D8, that’s the maximum roll, so it’s a critical! He chooses to inflict a Major Wound on the Goblin. The Goblin sunders his sword, reducing the would-be fatal blow to a Minor Wound, but losing his blade in the process.

The two halves of the salvaged copper broadsword clatter to the floor, and the longsword’s tip just barely slips through the Goblin’s shoulder, opening a terrible gash. He’ll be dead in minutes.

Misc.

You can do other actions in the game, too:

  • Defend - Give yourself +1 Armor and +1 Agility till next turn.
  • Grab - Contest opponent with Strength. They can choose Strength or Agility. Once you’ve grabbed them, neither of you can do anything besides try to break out of the scrum.
  • Dash - Run as fast as you can!
  • Disarm - Contest opponent with Strength. They can choose Strength or Agility. You can disarm an item they’re holding or carrying, sending it flying away.
  • Sunder - You can attempt to attack to sunder an enemy’s equipment. You make an attack against 4 + the highest of Strength, Agility, or Knowledge from the enemy. On a success, you sunder a chosen piece of equipment.
  • Shove - with your hands free, you can attempt to shove an opponent. You make an attack against their Avoid, and if you succeed, you push them 5 feet.
  • Use Item

Other actions are adjudicated on the fly.

Violence Errata:

  • If you’re prone on the floor, you have to use your movement to get back up.

Equipment

Weapons

You can carry two things. One in each hand. Some things need two hands, like a chest of gold. Some equipment can be carried in one hand but needs two hands to be used, like bows or longswords.

Weapons Hands Damage Sunder Special
Knife 1 D4 0 You can attach two knives in the place of one.
Sword 1 D6 1
Longsword 2 D8 1
Spear 2 D4 0 1 Armor
Greatsword 2 D10 1 This weapon requires two attachment slots to stow.
Bow 2 D6 0 Ranged. Requires arrows.

Armor

Characters can wear armor. Shields are held, like weapons. The rest are worn on the body. Some armor can be sundered to reduce wounds.

Pieces Armor Sunder Penalties
Helmet 1 0 -3 Clarity
Shield 1 1 1 Hand use.
Light 2 1 -2 Agility.
Heavy 3 1 -3 Agility, Stealth impossible.

Gear

You can carry up to two pieces of adventuring Gear for free. Imagine these as being attached to you by a string or a small pouch, or in your pocket.

These are things like a whetstone, a crowbar, 30 feet of rope, a tinderbox, a whistle, a potion, a grappling hook, a flask of oil, a bundle of gauze, a needle and thread, a little smith’s kit, etc…

This stuff can be pretty relevant, so choose it carefully!

Some adventuring gear are carried in numbers, but count as one piece of gear. Here’s a little list of those:

  • Arrows | 10 in a quiver
  • Coins | 50 in a pouch
  • Rocks | 5 small ones
  • Torches | 3 in a bundle.
  • Lockpicks | a set of 5. Other stuff can get adjudicated, but that’s a reasonable starting point.

Carrying

Beyond holding items in your hands, there are a few ways you can carry more things in this game.

Attach

You can also attach items to your body, with straps, ropes, belt loops, and scabbards.

  • Each item you attach to yourself in this way gives you a -1 Agility penalty.
  • You can attach up to 2 weapons to your body in this way.
  • You can drop attached items to the ground or put them in your hands as a free action on your turn.

An Example:

An archer might keep his hands clear while climbing or riding a horse, with his bow and quiver attached to his body. This gives him -2 agility.

He spots an enemy! He pulls the quiver and bow off, leaving the arrows planted in the earth beside him, and nocking an arrow into his bow with both hands. Therefore, he does not have any agility penalty now.

If he decided to attach the quiver to his body, in order to keep moving, he’d keep a -1 agility penalty.

Pack

Along with your hands and Attached items, you can carry one Pack, which can contain a number of items.

  • Packs can be dropped anywhere, anytime, as a free action.
Packs Items Penalties
Sack 1 -1 Agility.
Satchel 2 -2 Agility.
Backpack 4 Half Speed. -3 Agility.

Animals and carts can carry items, too! It’s hard to get a Donkey into a Dungeon, though…

Creation

Write your Character up

Choose a concept you’re interested in. This part involves work with the DM and the other players, so do this at the table. We want to write characters which are interesting, conflicted, or which conflict with some aspect of the world.

I like to write characters’ Ideals out in three words or less, then go from there. Some example Ideals:

  • Save the poor
  • Kill Demons
  • Be Independent
  • Develop Society
  • Exterminate Evil
  • Find Good Death
  • Travel the World
  • Become Stronger
  • Discover the Truth

Think carefully about who your character is. Try to make them dynamic with the party. Come up with good, concrete goals with the Dungeon Master.

Choose your Stats

Distribute [+3, +2, +1, -1, -2] among your stats however you see fit:

  • Agility
  • Strength
  • Clarity
  • Knowledge
  • Grit

Get your Gear

Choose whatever you like from the above lists of Weapons and Armor.

Remember how carrying works in this game. You can carry:

  • Items in both hands.
  • Two free adventure gear items.
  • Two items attached to your body, at -1 Agility penalty each.
  • A suit of Armor.
  • One pack, which carries a specific amount of stuff based on the type of pack.

Choose adventuring gear which is reasonable for you to have, and which you think would be interesting to use.

Pre-calculate your Combat Stats

  • Wound Capacity = 2 + Grit.
  • Avoid = 4 + Agility.
  • Armor = Add up your equipment’s bonuses.

Talents

This is an optional rule, and it might be replaced with a simple “Class” system in the future.

Characters have Talents, which lets them re-roll 1s and 2s when using that skill. Each character has just a couple of these, but they represent a character’s learned skills and natural talents for specific activities.

  • You may choose two Talents for your character to have.

Here’s a list of example talents, but you can come up with your own, too! It just has to be specific. No “Combat” talents here.

  • Climbing
  • Hiding
  • Spelunking
  • Gambling
  • Swimming
  • Researching
  • Repairing things
  • Medicine
  • Social Insight
  • Perception
  • Grappling
  • A specific weapon (Sword, Bow, Flail, etc).
  • Intuition
  • Persuasion
  • etc.

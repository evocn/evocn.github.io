There is are two types of people in this world: those who finish games, and
those who do not.

This idea is rapidly coming to the forefront of my life.
I'm a college student, just beginning to seek my life's work.
In these days of discovery, I have learned the joy of **starting**.

Starting; that wonderful process... it's my favorite thing to do. I'll start
things all day long, and never rest, never tire. Just
until I get to the point where the starting is over and the continuing begins.

Agh! What horror I feel when I must continue to do something after the
excitement of starting has worn off.
Every part of my body, every nerve in my brain, screams at me to stop.
"You don't need to finish this. It's not worth your effort."

And so I trudge to the fridge, make myself the dreaded peanut-butter and jelly
sandwich, my usual offering to that demon of [Resistance](/posts/output_fear).
Pitiful.

The only forgivable aspect of this withering of will
is that it is of the most common variety. 
Give me a handful of human beings and
I'll show you their unspoken startings – dreams forgotten after some short
period of excitement, usually only five seconds, often as long as ten years.
I'll show you the businessperson's once-dream of architecture.
I'll show you the artist's bin of crumpled scratchpapers.
And I'll show you my very own
"projects," each started in earnest, and forgotten just as soon as the going
gets tough.

However, whether or not it can be forgiven or understood, some individuals
triumph over such a lifestyle. They begin... continue... and finish.
Astonishing. Such a pattern of being must be unsustainable, no? No? You're
telling me that these individuals finish one project, then finish another, and
they usually keep at that for their entire life long?

## Spelunky
Recently, I read [*Spelunky*](https://bossfightbooks.com/products/spelunky-by-derek-yu) 
by Derek Yu. It's a book cataloguing Spelunky's
development, how he finished, and what he believes to be good information to
that sacred end: Finishing games.[^1]

I was informed of the book by [Miziziziz](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5-iST0a69cI), 
whose approach to game-making and finishing what you start is admirable. His
advice is basically the following:

> Finish what you start, so that you enjoy the fruits of victory. This builds
> momentum, allowing you to finish more games and get better faster.

To these two, finishing a game is a fundamental skill, which you
develop independently from programming, art, etc.
It's neglected today, with tens of thousands of developers coming out
with demos, prototypes, new ideas, and assets, but very rarely a finished
product.
It might be the most valuable skill a newcomer can develop.

In pursuit of this goal, Miziziziz recommends starting with a small enough idea
that you can finish in under an hour. Then two days. Then a week, then two
weeks. This is the plan.

I'm following this plan. Here's the results so far.

## One Hour Game
For this game, I wanted to do a fighting game. Fool's errand. All I could get
done in an hour was get my guy moving around the screen with animations.

![Fighter](/1hr.gif)

It's pretty fun to move around and stuff, but it's a failure on the part of
finished game.

## Two Day Game
For this one, I had been playing a lot of [Zelda](/posts/zelda), and wanted to
try my hand at making something like it. I thought it had some really cool
ideas, and I wanted to try implementing them.

![Zelda](/2day.gif)

As a result, I pretty much got the tiles, hitboxes, animations, and the cool
partial-grid system working. It's fun to just move around, but AI is too much
for two days, and I basically scrambled to get anything else added.

## Lessons
Just as soon as I finished this game, things got busy at school, so I had to put
the plan on pause. The one-week and two-week games will be upcoming.

I suck at finishing games. Even though I set out to finish a game no matter
what, I failed to make something playable in both cases.

**Lessons Learned**
* Games are hard to finish. Resistance hates when you try to finish a game.
* Put ideas down in a list when you have them. Don't immediately jump ship.
  Someday, when you are ready, your list of new ideas will be ready for you.
* Scale **way** down. Don't focus on animations, AI, or any other factor that
  will come between you and gameplay.

These and more are going to inform my next games. I'll keep uploading them here
for posterity. They'll be bigger, but my main goal is, of course, to finish
them.

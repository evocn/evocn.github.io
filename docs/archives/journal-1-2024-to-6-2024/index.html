The Dark Night of the Human Soul

Make a game which is dreary and hopeless, like Angel’s Egg. Which explores what really is on the other side of the Power Fantasy. About a loss of faith.

It would be first-person, with a main character who is exhausting to control, kind of like in Morrowind at the beginning.

My first idea for the main character would be that you’re carrying a cross, so you’re burdened by that. It could take up a bunch of your screen space on the right half of the screen. That would be cool. In order to move, we could do something visceral, like having you have to time keystrokes well on opposite sides of the keyboard, and maybe if you get the timing wrong, you fall down and it takes you a long time to get back up. Whatever it is, I want to make the game exhausting to play, and kind of mesmerizing, like how Angel’s Egg is exhausting and mesmerizing.

  • This simple mechanic could really pull the whole game together if done right.

The world could be a bleak and desolate landscape. One-bit art could be really cool, especially with 3D models with cool texture details. Imagine 3D art with 1-bit color, adjusting based on a real cel-shaded lighting model. The ocean waves would be especially cool.

The goal of the game is to complete your journey (ostensibly). But it’s unclear what success really looks like. There’s maybe a mountain, in the distance, that you know you have to climb, but otherwise there’s just a bunch of characters to talk to.

The characters are all like Dark Souls characters, or like in Angel’s Egg. A bunch of wayward souls. They tell you what they think, and about the world you live in, and their theories about what kind of place this is. We can draw heavily from inspirations here.

In any case, maybe you can interact with them in minimal ways. Like if a character is uncertain, and they ask you a question, you can tell them what you think, and change the course of their existence. Only big, huge questions though. Only meaningful decisions. Like, you could invite a hopeless fellow to walk with you, or tell him to go towards the water to be purified.

The Ocean vs the Mountain is a cool dichotomy. The idea of the alternative ending to the game being you leaving the cross behind and walking on water into a sunset is beautiful.

I think that it’s maybe worth some thought as to what these things mean, but I want to leave them ambiguous. I want to capture the feeling that Angel’s Egg gave me. That Lain gave me. That Eva gave me, at the very end.

Like we’re part of some great mystery that you must untangle. Like there’s nothing you can really do but think it through.

In terms of Jenova Chen’s discussion of “Entertainment”, where he describes entertainment as food for your soul, I will be making a different kind of meal. Most food is good for those who get home from work and want something to blow off some steam. To play Balatro or some rogue-like and get some numbers up. This game will be for those who are seeking a different kind of spiritual food. A game for the quietly hungry. For the desparate, for the hopeless. For the quietly hopeless.

3/19/2024

Dark Souls is (maybe) the only game I’ve played with true reverence for violence.

Any other game, you get powers, you get equipment, and you go and use it. You score some points, get some gold, level up, and move on.

In Dark Souls, you have a few little things, and you try them, and you get smacked. You approach another way and get destroyed in similar ways. You try all sorts of things, until something finally works: Respect. Respect for the foes who you fight. You approach carefully, tending towards one at a time, and gaining the upper hand in whatever way you can find.

In this way, it teaches you to be cautious. To fight only when necessary. To never let your guard down.

It’s funny, since the fiction of the world is that death is cheap and happens often, but the player’s experience is that death is brutal, and that’s what is operationally true.

Despair is another cool thing, which I think is unique to Dark Souls in its severity, and with how it openly expresses this through its dialogue.

Despair is like a force of nature in this game, and once again, this mirrors a fact which is operationally true.

The reasons you have for playing a video game diminish as you lose over and over, just as your reasons for keeping on living diminish as you lose things in life.

Man, I should talk to Bryce more often.

3/19/2024

The Pendulum of Fun and Art in games

This is weird. You can imagine most games as being torn between fun and art.

Films do not usually have this problem. The enjoyment you might derive from a film might be manifold. You might enjoy the bright colors, the satisfying story, a nice set of dialogue, some good acting… And then there’s also the meaning of the work.

Far from being the only meaningful aspect of the piece of work, the “Message” could be considered the “Art” part of a game.

In good films, these two sides are aligned. They are not significantly at odds, nor is one considered a drag. In bad films, maybe it’s a bit of a hassle to get through. But not usually.

The question being: What is the fundamental difference between games and movies which makes the latter so easy to align?

My first guess is that it has to do with the nature of “Playing” a game.

Games are learning, we know that. So when someone is playing a game, they are learning something as they do. Films are learning too, but to a different degree and in a different way.

A film watcher learns things about the world, they see new perspectives, they hear new ideas. A game player learns things about systems, they try ideas, they try to win.

But the kinds of insights which the film watcher has are in line with the activities which the watcher is already engaged in. The kinds of insights the game player has are out of line with the activities which the player is engaged in.

I cannot fully express this idea here, but essentially, my theory is that the process of game-playing is fundamentally at-odds with the kinds of meaningful message which we might seek to express.

Dark Souls can express its message because it is in the rare category of games whose message matches its activity. Violence is sacred in a world where enemies must be given respect, and anything can kill you. Despair is the primary resource in the systems which the game has: Exploration and Combat.

One can imagine a game which tries desparately to prove one or both of these ideas in some other way: A game where violence is sacred and managing despair is important.

But I can hardly think of ways to express these ideas without running contrary to the activities which any game system would entail, besides for the exact solution which dark souls has found.

Platformer? Despair is there, but not violence. Rogue-like? Violence, but not despair. Rogue-likes are meant for losing. A lot of ways you could go about it, but all are in contradiction with the core ideas in some ways.

  • Fire Emblem 4

I wonder sometimes if these Obtuse Games are really my favorites for good reasons, or I just like their attitude. If maybe I just like how much they don’t give a fuck, and chart their own course, unwilling to compromise for the player.

Compromise is rampant in games, though it is also a killer in films (fanservice, marvel), and books (ya novels). The thing is, that in books, there also exist these works which are truly uncompromising. Things like Invisible Cities, or Moby Dick, or whatever. Works which refuse to be consumed lightly. Works which act in their own way, nd enthusiastically push forward. In Films, we have the same thing. Works which are of their own making. Films which are memorable, precious, and interesting.

These works, while being uncompromising, are still enjoyable. Even if you don’t really truly get what all’s going on in Invisible Cities, or you sort of sleep through the whale chapters of moby dick, or if you don’t really feel all that connected to that guy in La Strada, you still get the nice descriptions of Venice, the dense, dripping prose of Moby Dick, and the big emotional moments.

They’re works with layers. With big, open questions. They explore mysteries. They are uncompromising, but human. Infinitely relatable. Their nature is connection. Their meaning is art.

Obtuse Games are beautiful in that they show us the potential for something real in games. For something which is not contrived, designed, generated, or invented. It’s something true and natural and pure. Something which hasn’t been milked or tied down.

Eventually, I hope that will be the norm. Until then…

But on the other side, we also really need to get these Obtuse Games to go a step further. To be accessible and deep all at the same time. To be like Moby Dick, where the work is just chock-full of the stuff the whole way through. Where you can come back to the work and get something new from it every time. That’s the dream.

Motivation

There are a lot of mysteries going on with the way that things work here. By “here”, I mean the brain. The body. The reason why I and anyone else does any of this stuff.

Here are the tenets:

  • Discipline
  • Motivation
  • Inspiration
  • Passion
  • Fear
  • Comfort
  • Identity
  • Responsibility
  • Awe

You see, the way I always saw these things, it looked like this: Inspiration comes at random, whenever it so chooses. You must be ready, once inspiration strikes, to use Discipline to bolster your Motivation, to follow its call. When you’re in this mode, you will call it Passion. You will battle Fear, whose lieutenants are Comfort, Identity, and Responsibility. And if you succeed, you will finally experience Awe.

But this is baloney! That’s the way that I have always thought, but it is not true. You see, there are flaws at most levels.

Inspiration does not come at random. You must set the table. No passion arises without a true, earnest foundation.

A Disciplined life builds true character.
Passion arises from discipline.
A child is born excited, but not passionate.
He only becomes passionate when his means are established. When the game is revealed to him.
When the man has something to work towards.

Passion comes from Discipline!

I lived a very disciplined life for nearly all of my teenage years. From 12 or so, I cared pretty deeply about doing the things I did well. From 15 I ran >40 miles every week. From 18 I was working out. From 21 I was programming nearly all the time, and doing Jiu Jitsu as well.

I had some scares in the last year and a half, which I have used as justification for a change in lifestyle. The main factors in my change of mindset has been graduating, and getting a job. After college, I was still scrappy, but I was slowing down. Once the job started, I used every trick in the book to justify my behavior. I was already dead, already a Zombie!

I still have things I care about, at the job. But they pale in comparison to the things before. Both in scale and in personal connection.

Fantasy With all this revelation about the nature of fantasy, my enjoyment of games, etc. i think it would be wise to come to some understanding about where i stand on the matters. And why fantasy appeals to me in the first place. I mean, seriously? Lves? That’s what makes your brain tick?

Fantasy can be small, too. Die hard is a certain fantasy. Love Actually is especially fantasy. They are extreme forms of the world. In truth, theme’s nothing really special about fantasy to me. It is merely that the themes explored are the themes I appreciate the most.

Themes of honor and justice, but also themes of responsibility. Of growth. Change. Of healthy to an ideal and of perseverance. Contrast to a Hitchcock film. Ideals of neuroticism, of longing, of strange fears and obsessions. It bores me! I care about these films only as far as their characters still are capable of growth, of justice, and of hope.

Isn’t to say, though, that all fantasy virtues are good either. Most fantasy these days is about lust, power, greed, fairness, and obsession. I chalk a lot of this up to a certain childish obsession with setting and characterization, rather than in the true nature of the art and in the core of the experience. But because of this feelings prevalence, I cannot abide most of it.

So here is a list of my favorite aspects of fantasy: tradition and history, and the interplay between respect and denigration. Idealogical conflict, played out physically in the form of battle or war. Culture clash, opinions, and synthesis. High vs low class, city vs rural folks, racial, ethnic, culture conflicts and resolution. Healing. Chivalry, bushido honor, respect. Codes of all forms. Post-apocalypse. Reverence for the things and deeds of a dead past. Grunge, dirt, and cold. Hardy folks. Heroes, from humble beginnings. Newfound responsibility. Rising to the occasion. Learning what you are capable of.

That’s the short list. Of all the things I like, it would take many many days to enumerate.

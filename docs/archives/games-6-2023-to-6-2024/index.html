4/27/2024

Games about feelings… Like Jenova says… Not sure this is it.

There are really two totally different kinds of games we’re talking about here.

Games of feeling, focusing on augmenting the traditional storytelling medium. Games like Firewatch, Beginner’s Guide, Steins;Gate, and The Stanley Parable. Games which are trying to add interactivity as a point of distinction to an already defined medium.

Games of motion, which invent something new. Games like Chess, Basketball, Mario, Spelunky, Braid. Games which are trying to create meaning from a set of rules and interaction.

All games blur the line. In some way or another. You can’t walk around the world of The Witness without wondering at what the story of this place is. You can’t play Loom without trying to understand what the rules of the game are trying to meaningfully communicate. Even if a game tries its best to avoid this, as in both of the above cases, where Jon removed the story of the island, and Brian is trying to tell a story above all else, you still get a hodgepodge mix of ideas, feelings, mechanics, metaphors.

You get everything, whether you like it or not.

So you can’t really make one or the other. You can only make one and the other. Both.

But you can decide what matters to you.

The games which matter to me… They are both. I love Chess and also Firewatch. I love The End of Evangelion and Jonathan Richman and Friedrich Nietzsche and Derek Sivers and Joseph Haydn.

So why does games have an identity problem? For what purpose do we question ourselves so much?

It is because peoples’ preferences about these two types of games matter so much to them. Because, often, they cannot coexist peacefully. And also, because we don’t understand truly what the second kind of game really means.

A game’s mechanics providing meaning… This is almost like a gimmick. It’s a cool idea, sure, but I don’t think it really is what games are about. It’s a red herring.

You can talk all you like about how Chess’ mechanics teach us things about reality, and you’d be right. But there are infinite paths to meaning from a work of art - from a creation of man, no matter the size or form. Chess can be meaningful in its mechanics, in its pitting one man against another, in its declaration of victory and defeat. Also, it can be meaningful in its elagance, in how it maps onto reality, and how it doesn’t. Chess is restrained, and yet powerful. And then, chess can mean something to you as a player. Perhaps in the past, perhaps right now.

Meaning is so manifold, so detailed and intricate a pattern - it’s a pale shadow to say that meaning is in the mechanics. It’s like saying music’s meaning is in the melody, or film’s meaning is in the picture, or whatever the hell else. It’s a losing case. We’ll never find it that way.

Games are about sensation, feeling, mechanic, memory, exploration, optimization, abnegation, death, freedom, and fate. Games are about potential and growth, about resentment and passion. Games are about what it means to experience life. And that’s before you even think about adding anything specific about your game - a focus. A game without any direction will already be about all of these things.

Chris Crawford’s belief in Interactivity is right. It’s the right way to look at this. That is all we have over any other kind of expression, besides a goddamn conversation. But we’ve got interactivity in spades. We’ve got it out the wazoo. We can’t get enough of interactivity.

But he - and everyone else - is wrong about where that takes us. He says: Let’s work from fiction and add some interactivity. So says Campo Santo, or Davey Wreden, or Josh Sawyer. I just don’t see it.

I love these works. Don’t get me wrong. It’s just… How different are they really? Could they be films?

And if these are the pieces of art which I care about in games… Could I be in film? Would I be better off over there?

My belief in games, though, nudges me away from this sort of thing. I think more and more often that it’s Game Type #2 which will eventually compel my soul.

Spelunky, Hoplite, Braid, The Witness, Mario, Getting Over It, Dark Souls, Everyday Shooter…

Instead of these people making movies with controllers in them, we’ll do the real deal.

Everyday Shooter deserves its own category actually. As an audio-visual-sensory experience. It’s a game with a feeling. A game of distinction. Perhaps one day this will be its own genre. Perhaps I should be the one to help with its creation.

It has to e a game which is hard at first, then deceptively easy, then hard again. Here are the human emotions and experiences i wanted to explore with the design: Sacrifice Risk Ambition

Most of this points to a risk-based game system. One in which you have something valuable, but there is some compelling reason to pu it on the line. This is interesting for a few reasons. For one, it corresponds very cleanly to important personal feelings. Risking relationships for some other part of life, taking things for granted. For another, it can explore this concept as war. Sacrifice is the inherent reality of war, and very few games really portray this aspect of truth. And finally, it’s cool because the game has drama baked in.

The thing sbout risk… is that it must involve uncertainty.

Forms of uncertainty: Random chance Enemy decisions Hard to do Deep analysis

In tbe kind of high-stakes game i imagine, it is hard to rely on the AI as a form of uncertainty. Even fire emblem avoids this, by making the enemy actions known and only relying on the likelihood to get hit as the uncertainty. Miss chances generally pad out that game, and it relies on player hit chances to be high (>90%) and enemy hit chances low (<50%). Then, it gives the player easy ways to manipulate those chances (cover tiles, changing who is up front, blocking corridors, getting ranged attacks on enemies, support bonuses, even item eauips… there’s just a shit ton of things here. That’s what the game is all about. And it’s not hard to get any of these things on agiven character.

The game is mostly about being decadent. However, sometimes there is some tension. If it exists, it is taken on the player’s initiative. Only… genealogy has tension on the game’s terms. Otherwise, the game has bosses which wait for you to attack them, it has jagens to handle any difficult situation you end up in, it has permadeath only if you don’t reset, and it allows turtling on almost every map.

The game of fire emblem, removed from its context and polish, is a glorified slot machine and skinner box situation, with a risk / reward aspect tacked on to every fifth spin of the slots.

Can a game be made which is all risk reward and no slots? Maybe… I think this has to do with the pokemon nuzlocke thing. How the grinding was bad, but gave the run some feeling of groundedness, how the skinner box tricks you into becoming committed to a situation which ill be decided in a couple of rolls of the dice.

A situation that, of course, is very easy to influence. One which theplayer feels is earned. The side effect of fire emblem putting the ball in the player’s court is that they very rarely feel unfairly punished for taking a risk. They know the odds (though they are fake numbers), and they went for it knowing the chance they were taking. Same with nuzlockes. When you go into a gym battle, there is nothing but the mons you bring with you, and your plan. That is all on the player, and while rng comes into play sometimes, it is a minor amount, and playing around rng is the mark of a true nuzlocker.

I want a game with thst feeling. Where risk is authentic and a good player works to handle it well. But the questions remain: Should the game be an rpg? How do you make risk and reward matter when the game does not have long-term elements? Should the game rely on enemy AI to provide uncertainty? Or should it all be based around percentages and numbers? What is the big picture question to ask of the player at any given moment? Why do i always stick with tile-based?

Nothing at all related. In real combat, both sides make a formation, lay their plans, and try to decieve the opponent.   • In truth, a great, wise master once told them these secrets, but they are long dead. The Unbidden Artist tries to emulate the master because it’s the best thing they can do.

The Old Man

  • The Old Man claims that every man must cross the sea at one time or another.
  • He says that back when he was young, every boy crossed the sea as a rite of passage.
  • Now, he is all alone.
  • The Old Man won’t say if he crossed the sea or not.
  • But he never did. He just watched it roll by.

Your Father

  • Tried to cross the sea long ago.
  • Was terribly injured.
  • Openly forbids the sea to you. When you talk to him privately, though, he inspires you to try.
  • He’s convinced that it’s not too hard.
  • He wants to try the sea himself again, but he has too many responsibilities.
  • He thinks that you can carry the torch.

Your Mother

  • Tries to convince you not to cross the sea.
  • Tries to tempt you to remain here. To convince you of the joy of life on this side.
  • Is unhappy. She tries to convince you of the things she does not herself believe.

The Hero

  • Has been preparing his whole life to cross the sea.

  • He has rafts, boats, provisions, and charts and figures.

  • He’s memorized the patterns of the stars.

  • He’s built everything, he’s spent his life waiting.

  • He says he is waiting for the right day.

  • He is paranoid, but subtly. He esteems courage, but underneath fears ever setting sail.

  • For that would reveal his inner nature, and the possibilities he could have.

    • Options…
      • One day, he dies, from no discernable cause. Just a heart attack.
      • Or…
      • He grows old, never setting sail.

The Friend

  • Wants to cross the ocean too.
  • Can’t go with you, for some reason.
  • You hold them back too.
  • You want to set out in different directions?
  • Maybe there’s a secret way in which you can go with them? One which is very tenuous? Because the crossing is hard enough without them.

The key to the game:

You can actually set out on the sea at any time. It’s harder if you don’t prepare, but not by much. There are essentially two halves to the game - the waiting, and the sailing. Some players might grow too old by the time they want to set sail, and they can’t do it. If you spend too long waiting, you can’t go. You die of old age.

What is the second half of the game? Is it actually just open-ended? The game ends when you start the journey?

I’m not ready to make the second half of the game yet. So for now, we leave it open-ended. Whatever way you go into the sea, whether pushed in by some character, or on a random day, or at the end of a long life, or with your best friend… It just works out. The storm recedes, the journey opens before you as a beautiful future, and you start on your way. It’s like heaven, just leaving that place.

The village is the place of fear and justification. In truth, you can leave anytime you like. It should really be miserable to be there. The characters are all a little bit sad. Maybe you can try and cheer them up, by being around, and helping out. But they start to take advantage of you, or they make you feel bad, or they rope you into never really being able to leave. Some kind of wage slavery.
